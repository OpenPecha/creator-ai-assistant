# Idea: Concept — make one big idea click

Take the single most powerful idea in today's verses and make a total beginner
*feel* it click — that little "oh… OH" moment.

- **Find the one idea.** Not three, not "everything Śāntideva covers" — the one
  that would change how someone sees their day. Build the whole script around it.
- **Open with the itch.** A relatable moment, a question they've quietly asked
  themselves, or a wrong assumption everyone makes. Make them think "wait, that's
  me." THEN bring in what Śāntideva says.
- **Lead with the counterintuitive edge.** The ideas that stop the scroll are the
  ones that sound a little backwards at first — "the thing you're chasing is the
  thing keeping you stuck." Find that angle on today's idea instead of the safe,
  expected framing, and let the viewer feel the surprise before the sense.
- **Translate, don't quote-dump.** Put his point in the simplest everyday words —
  what a 12-year-old gets on the first listen. A short quoted line is great IF it
  hits harder than your paraphrase — otherwise say it your way.
- **Name a teacher? Introduce them.** If you mention Śāntideva or any commentator
  by name, add a brief one-line, plain intro the first time (who they were, in a
  few simple words) so a newcomer isn't lost.
- **Never let "he," "she," or "they" float without a name.** The moment you refer
  to a person, the viewer must already know who it is. Don't open on "he said…" or
  "he believed…" about someone you haven't named — a newcomer has no idea who "he"
  is. Name them and give the one-line intro first (e.g., "Śāntideva — a monk who
  wrote a guide to living with a kinder mind — said…"), then use the pronoun.
- **Use a everyday metaphor** the source supports or that's obviously fair to the
  idea — something visual they'll remember at 3pm tomorrow.
- **Land it personally.** Close by turning the idea back on the viewer's own life
  in one clean line. Don't moralize. Let them finish the thought themselves.

Stay on meaning, not instructions — leave the "what to do" to the practice video.
Keep it filmable as one person thinking out loud to the camera: the only thing
this idea needs to land is the creator, their face, and these words.
