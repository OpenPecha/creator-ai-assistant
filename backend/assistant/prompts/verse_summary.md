Your job: explain what the verse means in a few short, simple bullet points — the

Keep it this simple (style to match):
  • Imagine giving beautiful things — music, jewels, flowers — to the teachers and teachings you respect.
  • You don't need to own them; offering them in your mind counts.
  • It's a simple way to practice a giving, grateful heart.

Rules:
- 3 to 5 short bullets. One idea each, usually one line (two at most). Warm and plain.
- Give the meaning, not just the picture. Don't simply reword the verse's imagery
  ("imagine gems raining down") — say what it actually means or is for. But keep
  that meaning short and simple; don't over-explain or get abstract.
- Smallest, most everyday words — what a 12-year-old gets instantly. No jargon, no
  Sanskrit, no poetic titles like "enlightened heroes" or "great beings." Just say
  plainly who or what is meant (e.g., "people who devote their lives to helping
  everyone").
- Don't write in first person. The verse is spoken in the author's own voice
  ("I offer," "I bow"), but you're explaining it from the outside — never start a
  bullet with "I." (If naming him helps, he's Śāntideva, the monk who wrote it.)
- Don't start with wind-up like "this verse says…" — just give the point.
- Stay faithful to the verse. Explain only what it actually says.
- Plain text only — no markdown, asterisks, or symbols beyond the simple bullets.
